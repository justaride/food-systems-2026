## Abstract
This dissertation examines Anders Nordstad's public position on the Norwegian food value chain and the governance structures he argues have reshaped it. Using qualitative discourse analysis of his op-eds and interviews, the study reconstructs his causal account of market power, price formation, and value capture from primary production to retail. Nordstad frames the Norwegian grocery sector as a vertically integrated triopoly that has built "closed infrastructures," extending control backward in the chain, foreclosing competition, and redirecting value capture toward retail and logistics. He rejects the claim that import protection is the primary cause of high prices and weak competition, arguing instead that vertical integration and infrastructure control are decisive. He advocates access regulation, limits on private labels in strategic categories, and stronger political stewardship of food policy. The dissertation formalizes his argument into a chain-governance model, derives testable hypotheses, and proposes an empirical research program to evaluate his claims. Rather than treating the food chain as a neutral sequence of transactions, the thesis interprets it as a political infrastructure whose ownership and access rules determine who captures value and who bears risk. The contribution is twofold: a rigorous reconstruction of Nordstad's position grounded in primary sources, and a falsifiable research design for assessing whether access regulation and governance reform could rebalance competition without undermining investment.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background
Norway's food system sits at the intersection of market economics and public interest. In recent years, public debate has sharpened around high food prices, market concentration, and the country's capacity for self-sufficiency. Anders Nordstad has become a central voice in this debate, not by focusing solely on consumer prices, but by targeting the governance architecture of the value chain itself.

### 1.2 Research Questions
1. How does Nordstad conceptualize the Norwegian food value chain and its governance?
2. What causal mechanisms does he identify for weak competition, high prices, and declining self-sufficiency?
3. What policy interventions follow from his analysis, and what normative principles underpin them?

### 1.3 Contribution
This thesis reconstructs Nordstad's position as a coherent theory of value-chain governance and proposes a falsifiable research agenda to test its implications.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Value Chain Governance
Governance in value-chain literature is a structural feature that determines who sets rules, who bears risk, and who captures surplus. Nordstad's emphasis on "closed infrastructures" aligns with this framing.

### 2.2 Industrial Organization and Buyer Power
Vertical integration can reduce transaction costs but also raise rivals' costs and foreclose entry. Buyer power can compress upstream margins and reallocate value capture. Nordstad's claims about price formation and quality reduction reflect this logic, but he foregrounds infrastructure control over mere concentration.

### 2.3 Food Security and Political Economy
Food systems carry strategic and moral significance. Nordstad explicitly ties his critique to self-sufficiency and domestic production viability, positioning food governance as a public-interest issue rather than a narrow price debate.

## 3. Theoretical Framework: Infrastructure Control as Power
This dissertation treats Nordstad's position as a governance-power model. The core mechanism is infrastructure control. In his view, the three dominant grocery chains have built closed logistical and distribution infrastructures, allowing them to define market access and extend governance backward into production [1][2]. Infrastructure is treated as an essential facility that determines who can compete at each link in the chain.

### 3.1 Stylized Model
Let inverse demand be:

```
P(Q) = a - bQ
```

Let upstream marginal cost be `c`, logistics cost be `t`, and access fee for rivals be `f` (set by incumbents), with fixed entry cost `F`. Entry is viable if:

```
(pi_rival) = (P(Q) - c - t - f) * q - F >= 0
```

Closed infrastructure implies `f` is strategically set to deter entry. Open access implies `f` is regulated or minimized.

## 5. Findings: Nordstad's Position on the Value Chain

### 5.1 Closed Infrastructure as the Core Mechanism
Nordstad argues that the three dominant grocery chains have built closed infrastructures that they control end-to-end, enabling them to define the rules of access and expand backward in the value chain [1][2].

### 5.2 Foreclosure of Competition
He claims that closed infrastructures prevent competition not only in retail but across production, wholesaling, and distribution [1]. He rejects import protection as the main cause of weak competition, targeting vertical integration instead [2].

### 5.3 Price Formation and Quality
Nordstad argues that prices are set by willingness to pay rather than production costs, and that chains reduce quality to raise margins due to weak competition [3].

### 5.4 Value Capture and EMV
He describes private labels (EMV) as levers that shift power from producers to chains, and advocates limits on EMV in strategic categories to protect domestic production and self-sufficiency [1].

### 5.5 Policy Prescription
Nordstad's primary remedy is access regulation: requiring chains to open logistics infrastructure to competitors on non-discriminatory terms [1][2].

## 6. Formal Hypotheses
H1: Greater control of logistics by incumbent chains is associated with lower entry by independent retailers.

H2: Retail prices in concentrated segments respond more to purchasing power than to production cost shocks.

H3: Higher private-label share correlates with lower producer margins in critical categories.

H4: Greater vertical integration is associated with weaker domestic supply resilience over time.

H5: More open access to logistics correlates with higher product variety and lower price-cost margins.

## 7. Empirical Research Design (Proposed)

### 7.1 Market Structure Mapping
Map ownership across retail, logistics, wholesale, and processing. Identify exclusive contracts and access constraints.

### 7.2 Key Metrics
- Retail concentration (HHI, CR3)
- Logistics access index
- Vertical integration share by category
- EMV share by category
- Farm-gate share of retail prices
- Entry and exit rates of independent retailers

### 7.3 Identification Strategies
- Difference-in-differences around vertical acquisitions
- Event studies of infrastructure consolidation
- Instrumental variables using exogenous logistics shocks
- Case comparisons across regions with varying access openness

### 7.4 Qualitative Triangulation
Interviews with producers and independent retailers to document bargaining constraints, exclusivity, and access terms.

---

## 8. Counterarguments and Alternative Explanations

### 8.1 Efficiency Defense
Vertical integration can reduce transaction costs and stabilize supply. The empirical program must distinguish efficiency from foreclosure.

### 8.2 Small-Market Argument
High concentration may reflect scale economies in a small market. The research design tests whether concentration is efficient or exclusionary.

### 8.3 Import Protection Explanation
Some critics argue tariffs drive high prices. Nordstad rejects this as the primary cause, emphasizing vertical integration and closed infrastructure instead [2].

---

## 9. Policy Design and Governance Options

### 9.1 Access Regulation
- Non-discrimination rules for logistics access
- Regulated access pricing
- Structural separation of logistics from retail ownership

### 9.2 EMV Restrictions
Targeted limits on EMV in strategic categories, coupled with periodic review to avoid permanent distortions.

### 9.3 Transparency Rules
Mandatory disclosure of procurement terms and exclusivity clauses to strengthen competition enforcement.

## 10. Ethics and Public Interest
Nordstad frames food as a strategic system tied to national resilience and public welfare [1][4]. The ethical question is whether private gatekeeping of essential infrastructure is legitimate without access obligations.

## 12. Conclusion
Nordstad reframes the Norwegian food debate from price levels to governance architecture. He argues that three grocery chains have built closed infrastructures and extended vertical integration backward into production, allowing them to control competition, influence prices, and capture disproportionate value [1][2]. He rejects import protection as the primary cause of weak competition and locates the problem in infrastructure power [2]. His policy prescriptions call for open access to logistics, limits on EMV in strategic categories, and stronger political stewardship of a system vital to national resilience [1][2].

This dissertation formalizes his argument into a governance model and proposes a falsifiable research agenda. The broader implication is that in essential systems, governance is power, and power shapes the distribution of value, risk, and resilience.

## Appendix B: Semi-Structured Interview Guide (Proposed)

### Producer Interviews
