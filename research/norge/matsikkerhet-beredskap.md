## 1. The Security Dimension: Food as National Infrastructure

### 1.1 Riksrevisjonen's Critique (Oct 2023 / Jan 2024)
While Nordstad focuses on the *economic* failure of the food system, the Office of the Auditor General (*Riksrevisjonen*) has exposed a critical *security* failure that parallels his arguments.
*   **The Findings:** The report "Matsikkerhet og beredskap" concluded that Norway is **unprepared for a food crisis**. The government relies blindly on the assumption that imports will always function.
*   **Relevance to Nordstad:** This validates Nordstad’s long-standing warning that relying on "efficient" global supply chains (facilitated by the trading houses' import dominance) leaves the nation vulnerable. The oligopoly's relentless pursuit of "just-in-time" efficiency has stripped the system of necessary redundancy (e.g., grain storage).
*   **Synthesis:** The "efficiency" that the chains claim justifies their size is, in fact, a national security risk. The cost of their profits is the resilience of the nation.

### 1.2 "Soviet" Agriculture vs. Crisis Preparedness
*   **Gaasland’s Counter-Argument:** Gaasland argues for deregulation to lower prices.
*   **Riksrevisjonen’s Rebuttal:** The Audit Office implies that *more* state control/planning is needed for preparedness, not less. This creates a fascinating tension: The "Soviet" system Gaasland mocks is actually closer to what the Auditor General implies is necessary for security (grain reserves, planned self-sufficiency).

## 2. Legal Precedents: The "Taxi Model" for Groceries?

### 2.1 EFTA Court & Regulatory Control (2024)
New developments in the taxi sector provide a potential legal roadmap for the grocery sector.
*   **The Precedent:** The government reintroduced strict requirements for taxi licenses (duty to serve, central connection) to combat a "wild west" market. The EFTA Surveillance Authority (ESA) has challenged this, but the EFTA Court (2024 context) accepts that "societal control" can justify strict market interventions if the problem is severe enough.
*   **Application to Groceries:** This legal logic could theoretically be applied to the grocery oligopoly. If "social control" (food security, fair pricing) is deemed critical, the state *could* legally impose stricter licensing or unbundling requirements on NorgesGruppen/Coop/Rema without necessarily violating EEA competition rules, provided the "public interest" argument is strong enough.
