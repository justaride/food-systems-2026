# Case Study: The Dashboard Dilemma
**Status:** Research Compiled
**Date:** January 24, 2026

This document explores the history of the "Ops Room"—the attempt to manage complex systems via a dashboard. It contrasts two opposing philosophies: the **Human-Centric Cybernetics** of Project Cybersyn vs. the **Metric-Obsessed Fallacy** of Robert McNamara.

This directly informs the design philosophy for the Oslo Dashboard.

---

## 1. The Good: Project Cybersyn (Chile, 1971-1973)
*The attempt to build a "Central Nervous System" for an economy.*

### The Vision
Stafford Beer, a British cybernetician, was hired by Salvador Allende to manage the Chilean economy in real-time. He didn't want a Soviet-style top-down bureaucracy; he wanted a "Viable System Model" that allowed factories to self-regulate while coordinating globally.

### The Ops Room
*   **Design:** A hexagonal room (no "head" of the table = democratic).
*   **Interface:** 7 ergonomic chairs with buttons (not keyboards) to control screens. Designed for workers and ministers, not just techies.
*   **The "Alhedonic" Loop:** An alarm system that only alerted the center if a factory couldn't solve a problem itself within a set time. This respected **autonomy** (Subsidiarity).
*   **Lesson:** A dashboard should be a tool for *coordination*, not *control*. It should empower the edge, not just the center.

---

## 2. The Bad: McNamara's Fallacy (Vietnam War)
*The danger of confusing the Map for the Territory.*

### The Philosophy
Robert McNamara (Secretary of Defense, ex-Ford CEO) believed that everything could be managed by statistics.
*   *Premise:* "If you can't measure it, it doesn't exist."
*   *Metric:* **Body Count.** (Number of enemies killed = Progress).

### The Failure
*   **Proxy Error:** A dead body is a proxy for "winning," but it's not victory. You can kill 10 people and create 20 new insurgents (Schismogenesis).
*   **Gaming the System:** Commanders inflated counts to look good. The data became "Noise" (Garbage In, Garbage Out).
*   **Blindness:** He ignored qualitative factors (Morale, History, Culture) because they didn't fit in a spreadsheet.
*   **Lesson:** "The First Step is to measure whatever can be easily measured. The Second Step is to disregard that which can't be easily measured. The Third Step is to presume that what can't be measured isn't important. The Fourth Step is to say that what can't be measured doesn't exist. This is suicide." (Daniel Yankelovich).

---

## 3. Application to Oslo
*How we avoid the trap.*

| Feature | **Cybersyn Model** (Goal) | **McNamara Model** (Trap) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Flow** | Real-time feedback for *adjustment* | Static reports for *judgment* |
| **User** | The Worker/Citizen (Participatory) | The Bureaucrat (Top-Down) |
| **Metric** | "Viability" (Is the system healthy?) | "Efficiency" (Cost/Yield) |
| **Failure** | Alert triggers help | Punishment triggers lying |

### Design Directive
We must build a **Cybersyn for Oslo**.
1.  **Visualize the Invisible:** Make "Social Health" and "Ecological Integrity" visible so they don't get ignored (McNamara).
2.  **Feedback, not Control:** The dashboard shows the city *to itself*, allowing it to self-correct (Cybernetics).
3.  **Qualitative Data:** Don't just count people; map *relationships* (Schismogenesis/Polarization).
