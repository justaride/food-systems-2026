## 1. COMPETITION AUTHORITIES & KEY LEGISLATION (Per Country)

### 1.1 Norway (NO)

**Authority:** Konkurransetilsynet (Norwegian Competition Authority)
- Key legislation: **Konkurranseloven** (Competition Act)
- Food-specific: **Lov om god handelsskikk i dagligvarekjeden** (Act on Fair Trading Practice in the Grocery Chain, entered force 2021-01-01)
- Enforcement body for fair trading: **Dagligvaretilsynet** (Grocery Market Supervision Authority) — proposed shutdown in 2026 state budget, responsibilities to transfer to Konkurransetilsynet

**Market structure:** NorgesGruppen, Coop, and Rema 1000 account for ~95% of grocery turnover (triopolistic)

**Food security strategy:**
- White paper on total preparedness ("Totalberedskap") presented January 2025
- **Target: 50% food self-sufficiency by 2030**
- **Target: 3-month contingency grain stock by 2029**
- Strategy: "Combining forces against hunger - a policy to improve food self-sufficiency"
- Currently self-sufficient in meat; significantly less so in plant produce; net exporter of seafood

**Key recent actions (2023-2025):**
- **August 2024:** NOK 4.9 billion (~EUR 420 million) in fines on NorgesGruppen, Coop, and Rema 1000 for the "price hunter" case (sharing retail price information since 2011)
- **December 2024:** Parliament adopted legislative amendments establishing market investigation powers in the Competition Act (enters force July 1, 2025)
- **January 2025:** Published two-part margin study finding return on net operating assets is "significantly higher than one would expect" in a competitive market
- **2025-2026:** Proposed transfer of Lov om god handelsskikk enforcement from Dagligvaretilsynet to Konkurransetilsynet; Dagligvaretilsynet has had recruitment challenges, few cases, and zero decisions on violations

### 1.2 Denmark (DK)

**Authority:** Konkurrence- og Forbrugerstyrelsen (KFST) (Danish Competition and Consumer Authority)
- Key legislation: **Konkurrenceloven** (Competition Act)
- Food-specific: **Lov om urimelige handelsmetoder i landbrugs- og fødevareforsyningskæden** (Act on Unfair Trading Practices in the Agricultural and Food Supply Chain)
- Enforcer for UTP: KFST

**Market structure:** Salling Group (~35%), Coop Danmark (~25-30%), Dagrofa, Reitan Group, Lidl — top 5 control ~95% of sales

**Food security / climate strategy:**
- Denmark has food self-sufficiency of ~150% (net food exporter, especially pork and dairy)
- First country to develop a mandatory **climate label for food** (announced 2024)
- Focus on sustainable food production through CAP strategic plan

**Key recent actions (2023-2025):**
- **March 2025:** KFST meeting with large grocery chains to examine pricing policies, including use of algorithms for price-setting and bargaining power dynamics
- **January 2025:** Phase II investigation opened into Salling Group's acquisition of 35 stores from Coop Danmark
- **March 2025:** Acquisition approved for 33 stores (two excluded in Taastrup and Slagelse due to local competition concerns)
- **2024:** KFST analysis showed food prices increased less in Denmark than in comparable countries (2022-2024); gross margins fell during high inflation, then rose to level below pre-2022

### 1.3 Sweden (SE)

**Authority:** Konkurrensverket (Swedish Competition Authority)
- Key legislation: **Konkurrenslagen** (Swedish Competition Act, SFS 2008:579)
- Food-specific: **Lag om otillborliga handelsmetoder vid kop av jordbruks- och livsmedelsprodukter** (Act on Unfair Trading Practices in the Purchase of Agricultural and Food Products, entered force 2021-11-01)
- Enforcer for UTP: Konkurrensverket

**Market structure:** ICA (~36%), Coop (~19%), Axfood (~24% post-City Gross), Lidl (~5%) — highly concentrated with local market bottlenecks

**Food security / self-sufficiency strategy:**
- **Livsmedelsstrategin 2.0** (National Food Strategy 2.0) presented in 2025
- Current self-sufficiency rate: ~50%
- Goal: increase domestic food production, strengthen competitiveness, and food supply resilience
- Government assignment to Business Sweden for investment-promotion in food production

**Key recent actions (2023-2025):**
- **2024 (Report 2024:5):** Sector inquiry into the food industry found risk of inadequate competition and unjustified price increases (egg prices cited as example)
- **October 2024:** Axfood's acquisition of City Gross approved (100% ownership for SEK 2 billion); City Gross = ~3.7% of retail; Axfood = ~20.5%. Completed November 1, 2024
- **October 2023:** First Scandinavian UTP fine — SEK 5 million on Everfresh (Dole Group) for payment periods exceeding 30-day limit
- **2025:** Government inquiry on new competition tools including notification obligations for large companies in concentrated industries (report due early 2025)
- Identified shortage of suitable retail sites as barrier to competition; municipalities control planning

### 1.4 Finland (FI)

**Authority:** Kilpailu- ja kuluttajavirasto (FCCA) (Finnish Competition and Consumer Authority)
- Key legislation: **Kilpailulaki** (Competition Act, 948/2011)
- **Section 4a (2014 amendment):** Abuse of dominant position threshold set at **30% national grocery market share** — applies to both S-Group (~46%) and Kesko (~36%)
- Food-specific: **Elintarvikemarkkinalaki** (Food Market Act, 1121/2018) — amended 2021 to implement EU UTP Directive
- Enforcer for food market: **Elintarvikemarkkinavaltuutettu** (Food Market Ombudsman)

**Market structure:** S-Group (~46%), Kesko (K-Group) (~36%), Lidl (~10%) — combined S+K > 80%; three largest > 90%

**Food security / self-sufficiency:**
- Almost self-sufficient in basic foodstuffs (~80% overall)
- Pork and poultry: production covers domestic demand
- Beef: ~80% domestic production
- Strategy: "Food2030" (Ministry of Agriculture and Forestry) — Finnish consumers eating sustainably and ethically produced domestic food by 2030

**Key recent actions (2023-2025):**
- **October 2024:** Market Court imposed EUR 600,000 penalty on Valio for breaching merger condition (Heinon Tukku acquisition)
- S-Group and Kesko both have >30% shares, so Section 4a dominant position prohibition is active
- FCCA investigation into sales data sharing between Kesko Food and SOK (no further action taken, but Section 4a framework applied)
- **January 2026:** Regional State Administrative Agencies ending; tasks transferring to new Finnish Supervisory Agency

## 2. EU-LEVEL DIRECTIVES AFFECTING THE FOOD CHAIN

### 2.1 EU Directive 2019/633 — Unfair Trading Practices (UTP)

**Scope:** Bans 16 unfair trading practices in B2B agricultural/food supply chain relationships. Protects suppliers with turnover up to EUR 350 million against stronger buyers.

**EU-wide enforcement (2021-2024):**
- 4,500+ investigations opened
- ~1/3 of closed investigations found infringements
- 754 infringement cases; EUR 41.9 million in fines (2022-2024)
- Commission evaluation published December 2025
- Proposed revision (COM(2024)576) under discussion in European Parliament

**Nordic implementation status:**

| Country | Transposition | Over-implementation | Enforcer | Notable |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|----------|---------|
| **Norway** | Lov om god handelsskikk (2021) — predates and is broader than UTP Directive | Yes — general "good business practice" standard; covers all grocery chain relationships | Dagligvaretilsynet (moving to Konkurransetilsynet 2026) | Zero enforcement decisions; "fear culture" prevents complaints |
| **Denmark** | Act on UTP in Agricultural/Food Supply Chain (2021) | Yes — "fairness for all" principle; applies regardless of company size | KFST | Active monitoring; meetings with chains on pricing algorithms |
| **Sweden** | Lag om otillborliga handelsmetoder (2021-11-01) | Yes — 30-day payment period for ALL products (not just perishables) | Konkurrensverket | First Scandinavian UTP fine: SEK 5M on Everfresh (Oct 2023) |
| **Finland** | Food Market Act 1121/2018 amended (2021-11-01) | Moderate — 30% dominance threshold supplements UTP | Food Market Ombudsman (under Ruokavirasto) | Committee on Food Supply Chain Trading Practices active |

### 2.3 EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR)

**Revised timeline (after December 2025 simplification):**
- **Revised Regulation 2025/2650** published in EU Official Journal December 23, 2025
- **Large operators/traders:** Must comply from **December 30, 2026** (12-month delay from original)
- **Small operators (<50 employees, <EUR 10M turnover):** Must comply from **June 30, 2027**
- Micro/small primary operators: Simplified one-off declaration only
- Downstream operators: No separate due diligence statement; must retain records for audits
- **Printed products removed** from scope
- **Commission review:** Report on impact and administrative burden due by April 30, 2026

**Products in scope:** Wood, coffee, cocoa, rubber, oil palm, soy, cattle/beef, and derived products

**Norway-specific:**
- Norway has decided to incorporate EUDR into the EEA Agreement
- **Exception:** Agricultural products and exports excluded (outside EEA scope)
- For high-risk products like beef and soya: Government exploring **national regulations** separately
- Norway may receive extra time for EEA incorporation process

### 2.4 EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR)

**Regulation (EU) 2025/40** — adopted December 19, 2024; published January 22, 2025

**Key dates:**
- **February 12, 2025:** Entry into force
- **August 12, 2026:** General application in all EU member states (directly applicable, no national transposition needed)
- **August 2028:** Uniform recycling labeling requirements
- **January 2030:** Only packaging with recyclability grades A-C may be marketed; minimum recycled content thresholds for plastic packaging
- **January 2035:** Recyclability must be demonstrated in practice at scale
- **January 2038:** Only grades A and B permitted

**Goals:** All packaging reusable or recyclable by 2030; drastic reduction in environmental/health impacts

**Nordic relevance:**
- DK, SE, FI: Directly applicable as EU regulation
- NO: Will need to be incorporated into EEA Agreement (packaging waste is within EEA scope)

## 4. NORWAY-SPECIFIC EEA CONSIDERATIONS

### 4.1 What the EEA Covers (Food Chain)

Norway, as an EEA/EFTA state, is **obligated to follow EU legislation on food and veterinary matters**. This means:

**Applies through EEA:**
- General Food Law (Regulation (EC) 178/2002)
- Food safety and hygiene regulations
- Food additives, enzymes, flavorings (EC Regulation 1333/2008)
- Plant protection products (EU Regulation 1107/2009)
- Nutrition and health claims (Regulation (EC) 1924/2006)
- Food contact materials regulations
- Organic production rules
- EUDR (with agricultural product exclusion — see below)
- PPWR (packaging within EEA scope)
- Competition law framework (EEA competition rules mirror EU)

**Norwegian food legislation** is largely harmonized with EU through "Matloven" (Food Act) and implementing regulations.

### 4.2 What the EEA Does NOT Cover

**Excluded from EEA:**
- **Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)** — Norway runs its own agricultural policy with significantly higher domestic subsidies and import tariffs
- **Common Fisheries Policy (CFP)** — Norway issues own fishing quotas; retains restrictions on ownership/establishment in fisheries
- **Customs Union** — No common external tariff; Norway sets own tariffs on food imports (often significantly higher than EU tariffs for domestically produced commodities)
- **Agricultural trade** — Free circulation of agricultural products is exempt; trade governed by Article 19 of the EEA Agreement (bilateral preferential trade) and Protocol 3 (price compensation system for processed agricultural products)
- **Tax/VAT policy** — Norway sets own food VAT rates (reduced rate of 15% on food)

### 4.3 Practical Differences

| Area | EU Member States (DK/SE/FI) | Norway (EEA/EFTA) |
|------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Agricultural subsidies | CAP-bound | Own policy; higher per-unit support |
| Import tariffs (food) | Common EU tariff | Own tariffs; often higher on domestic staples |
| UTP Directive 2019/633 | Must transpose directive | Own "Lov om god handelsskikk" (predates and broader than UTP) |
| EUDR | Directly applicable | Must be incorporated into EEA; agricultural products excluded; national rules for beef/soy |
| Farm to Fork | Full applicability | Food safety parts apply; agricultural policy parts do not |
| PPWR | Directly applicable as regulation | Must be incorporated into EEA Agreement |
| Food safety regulation | Directly applicable | Incorporated through EEA; near-identical rules |
| Fisheries regulation | Common Fisheries Policy | Own policy; not subject to EU quotas |

### 4.4 The "Gap" Problem

Norway's EEA position creates a regulatory **patchwork**:
- Food safety: Fully harmonized with EU
- Food market regulation: Own (arguably stronger) UTP law but weaker enforcement
- Agricultural trade: Protected by high tariffs, which contributes to higher consumer prices but supports domestic farming
- CAP exclusion means Norway can maintain higher agricultural subsidies without EU budget constraints, but also lacks access to EU agricultural market support mechanisms
- EUDR: Applies for forestry/industrial products but requires separate national action for agricultural commodities like beef and soy

## 6. KEY CROSS-CUTTING THEMES

1. **Concentration everywhere, tools vary:** All four Nordic grocery markets are oligopolistic (3-5 players controlling 80-95%). Policy responses differ: Finland uses a hard 30% dominance threshold; Norway has a separate fair trading law; Sweden/Denmark rely on EU-style UTP transposition with national over-implementation.

2. **Enforcement gap:** Norway's "Lov om god handelsskikk" is arguably the most comprehensive legal framework but has the weakest enforcement (zero decisions). Sweden achieved the first Nordic UTP fine only in 2023. The "fear culture" among suppliers suppresses complaints across all markets.

3. **Self-sufficiency divergence:** Denmark (150%) and Finland (80%) are structurally secure. Norway and Sweden (both ~50%) face genuine food security challenges, prompting explicit government strategies (Norway's Totalberedskap, Sweden's Livsmedelsstrategin 2.0).

4. **EEA creates a Norwegian paradox:** Norway has the highest food prices in the Nordics partly because EEA exclusion of agriculture allows protective tariffs and high subsidies — which supports domestic farming but insulates the triopolistic retail structure from import competition.

5. **Circular economy convergence:** All four countries target 50% food waste reduction by 2030 (Sweden's interim target is 20% by 2025). The EU PPWR (from August 2026) and binding food waste targets (from 2030) will create a unified regulatory floor. Finland's bioeconomy strategy is the most mature; Denmark leads on circular economy action planning.

6. **Regulatory momentum accelerating:** 2024-2025 saw unprecedented activity — Norway's NOK 4.9B fine, new market investigation powers, proposed merger of enforcement bodies; Sweden's first UTP fine and sector inquiry; Denmark's algorithm investigation; Finland's Valio penalty. The trend is toward stronger, more interventionist regulatory postures across all four countries.
